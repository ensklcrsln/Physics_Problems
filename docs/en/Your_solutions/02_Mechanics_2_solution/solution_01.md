## 1. Gravitational Dependence

### (a) Period on the Moon

The period of a simple pendulum is given by:

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

This equation tells us that the period depends on the inverse square root of gravity:

$$
T \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}
$$

On the Moon:

$$
g_{moon} = \frac{g_{earth}}{6}
$$

So:

$$
T_{moon} = T_{earth} \sqrt{\frac{g_{earth}}{g_{moon}}}
$$

$$
T_{moon} = 4 \cdot \sqrt{6} \approx 9.8 \text{ s}
$$

**Detailed Explanation:**
- The gravitational acceleration $g$ determines how strongly the pendulum is pulled back toward equilibrium.
- A smaller $g$ means a weaker restoring force.
- Because the restoring force is weaker, the pendulum moves more slowly through its arc.
- This increases the time required to complete one full oscillation.
- The square root relationship means the effect is not linear, but still significant.

---

### (b) Length for $T = 1$ s

Starting from:

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

Solve for $L$:

$$
L = \frac{gT^2}{4\pi^2}
$$

Substitute values:

$$
L = \frac{9.81}{4\pi^2} \approx 0.248 \text{ m}
$$

**Detailed Explanation:**
- We rearrange the formula to isolate $L$, because we want to design a pendulum with a specific period.
- The period increases with length, meaning longer pendulums swing more slowly.
- Here, we calculate the exact length required for a 1-second oscillation.
- This is the principle behind pendulum clocks.

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

### Formula 2: Period–Gravity Relationship

$$

$$
